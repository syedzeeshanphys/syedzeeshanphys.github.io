The Black Hole War

By Syed Noorulla Sha Quadri Article 7 of 7
"If you feel you are in a black hole, don't give up. There's a way out."
— Stephen Hawking

What would happen if you threw someone into a black hole? According to Einstein's general theory of relativity, the person would simply keep falling until they reached the singularity — where spacetime collapses and very bad things happen. But viewed through the lens of quantum mechanics, something different occurs: when the person reaches the event horizon, they are absorbed by it, radiation escapes, and eventually the black hole itself evaporates entirely.

These two pictures are irreconcilable — and that tension sits at the heart of one of the most spectacular debates in modern physics.

The information paradox

Everything in the universe carries information, encoded in the specific arrangement of its particles. When matter falls into a black hole and the black hole eventually evaporates, the question becomes unavoidable: where does that information go?

Stephen Hawking proposed that the information is simply lost — that pure general relativity permits nothing to escape a black hole, information included. If the black hole evaporates, the information vanishes with it. This idea ignited what became known as the Information Paradox.

Many physicists — including Leonard Susskind and John Preskill — disagreed sharply. They argued that information loss directly violates quantum mechanics, which holds that information can never be destroyed. Accepting loss would introduce dangerous inconsistencies between the two greatest theories in physics. Resolving the paradox, they argued, was not optional — it was essential for any unified understanding of the universe.

Proposed resolutions

Radiation carries the information

In 2019, a significant breakthrough came through two sets of papers — one by Netta Engelhardt and colleagues Almheiri, Marolf, and Maxfield, another by Penington. Using semiclassical gravity, they showed that the radiation emitted by an evaporating black hole eventually encodes all the information that fell in. Their analysis successfully passed a crucial test called the Page Curve, which tracks the entanglement of radiation over the black hole's lifetime — suggesting that information is conserved after all.

Information lives on the horizon

Gerard 't Hooft and Leonard Susskind proposed the Holographic Principle, inspired by Jacob Bekenstein's earlier discovery that black hole entropy scales with the area of the horizon rather than its volume. The Holographic Principle suggests that any information absorbed by a black hole is stored on its two-dimensional surface — like a hologram encoding a three-dimensional world. Taken further, this idea implies that our entire universe may itself be a kind of hologram.

Wormholes and other universes

A third possibility: the information travels through the black hole into another universe entirely, carried by wormholes threading through the geometry of spacetime. Theorists continue to debate how literally these wormholes should be taken — they appear deep in the equations, but their connection to physical reality remains uncertain. We still don't know what happens to that information.

The Black Hole War is not merely a technical dispute between physicists. It is a confrontation between two pillars of modern science — general relativity and quantum mechanics — and a reminder that our best theories, however successful individually, have not yet been reconciled into a single coherent picture.

The answer, when it comes, will likely reshape our understanding of space, time, and the very nature of information in the universe.
