AI in Science: What Einstein Couldn't Predict

By Syed Noorulla Sha Quadri Article 5 of 7

Albert Einstein transformed our understanding of the universe. His equations reshaped our grasp of time, space, and reality itself. But one force Einstein could never have conceived — not gravity, not quantum mechanics — was Artificial Intelligence.

And now, AI is not just powering recommendation engines or writing essays. It's venturing into the holy realms of scientific discovery — breaking through barriers, solving equations, and quietly questioning the role of human intuition in physics.

From thought experiments to machine learning

Einstein was famous for his thought experiments — imagining himself riding alongside a beam of light. He was intuitive, imaginative, and a genius. Today's AI is something different. It processes petabytes of data and recognises patterns that we simply cannot.

In 2023, ETH Zurich researchers developed AI software that could discover physical laws from raw, unprocessed data — without being taught any science first. It wasn't told about gravity. It learned it by watching things fall. If that isn't remarkable, consider that AI systems now generate hypotheses, design experiments, and even compose research papers.

Not a tool — a collaborator

Historically, scientific progress followed a linear path: observation, hypothesis, experiment, conclusion. AI has disrupted that chain entirely. It can reverse-engineer physical laws from experimental results, accelerate simulations that once took months, and dissect data with relentless efficiency. Consider a few examples:

  • Particle physics: AI software helps identify rare particle collision events at CERN's Large Hadron Collider.
  • Materials science: AI predicts novel compounds with desired properties before they are even synthesised.
  • Astrophysics: AI filters cosmic noise to find new exoplanets or detect gravitational waves.

Einstein would not have anticipated this transformation — not through any lack of imagination, but because AI fundamentally changes what it means to do science.

What happens to human intuition?

Here is the twist: if AI can learn physical laws directly from data, what role remains for human intuition? Do we become obsolete, or do we become co-creators?

Some argue AI can venture where the human brain cannot — into eleven-dimensional simulations or vast unstructured datasets. Others worry about the erosion of the creative, gut-driven reasoning that drove Newton, Curie, and Einstein. But perhaps this isn't a competition at all. Think of AI not as a rival, but as a multiplier — a machine that helps physicists test revolutionary ideas faster, explore chaos theory more deeply, or finally make progress on quantum gravity.

"The eternal mystery of the world is its comprehensibility."
— Albert Einstein

Ironically, we are now building machines that learn faster than us, yet sometimes cannot explain why. AI will never "know" the universe in a conscious sense. But it can guide us toward knowledge we'd never reach alone.

We don't have to be Einsteins anymore to be revolutionary scientists. But we do need to ask the right questions, learn to read what the machines are telling us, and keep the scientific spirit alive. Because at the end of the day, AI can accelerate discovery — but it's the human mind that gives discovery its meaning.
