The Difference Between Knowing and Understanding

By Syed Noorulla Sha Quadri Article 6 of 7
"Any fool can know. The point is to understand."
— Albert Einstein

Richard Feynman was one of the greatest physicists of the 20th century — one of my favourites — known not only for his Nobel Prize-winning contributions to quantum mechanics, but for his unique approach to knowledge itself. For Feynman, there was a critical distinction between merely knowing information and truly understanding it. That distinction mattered profoundly, both for meaningful learning and for intellectual honesty.

The problem with just "knowing" facts

Feynman was critical of what most people called learning — the memorisation of facts and formulas to produce answers on demand. To him, this was superficial and fragile. Students might learn to recite equations in physics without understanding why those equations work or what they reveal about the physical world.

He compared it to carrying around a dictionary: you know what the words "momentum" or "inertia" mean in the sense that you can define them, but you don't actually know what they do. Real understanding means seeing how momentum behaves in the real world, how it connects to force and energy, why it matters. Facts without connection, he argued, are not knowledge — they're furniture.

The Feynman technique

The journey from knowing to understanding is captured in what has become known as the Feynman Technique — a four-step method he used to test and deepen his own comprehension:

  1. Choose a concept that interests you — something you want to genuinely understand.
  2. Explain it in simple terms, as if you were teaching a child. No jargon allowed.
  3. Identify the gaps. Where your explanation breaks down is where your understanding breaks down. Go back to the source.
  4. Reflect and simplify. Revise your explanation until it is both accurate and clear.

The principle underneath it all: if you cannot explain something simply, you do not yet understand it. Complexity is often a disguise for confusion.

Understanding with curiosity

Feynman often illustrated the difference between knowing and understanding with concrete examples. If someone asked why light bends when passing from one medium to another, he would not simply quote the law of refraction. He would trace the physics, make the principles tangible, help the listener feel what was happening beneath the surface.

This is a useful reminder in an age where information is abundant and instant. Having access to a fact is not the same as understanding it. We are increasingly at risk of mistaking retrieval for comprehension — and Feynman's philosophy is a clean corrective.

"You must not fool yourself — and you are the easiest person to fool."
— Richard Feynman

Knowledge without understanding is weak. A well-understood concept, by contrast, becomes a living asset — one that opens doors to new ideas, new connections, new creativity. Feynman's philosophy is ultimately an invitation: be curious about the why and the how, not just the what. That is where learning actually begins.
