Is Time Real? The Strange Physics of Time

We say that time passes. Time says nothing — it's we who pass.

Syed Noorulla · 7 min read · Article 8 of 8

We all know a great deal about time, yet isn't it mysterious in some way? We plan our days around it, use it as a yardstick for our lives, and feel as though we're constantly competing with it. But what if time is not what we think it is? What if it doesn't even exist at the most basic level?

Some of the most recent ideas in physics suggest precisely that — our cosmos is not fundamentally based on time. Instead, time might be a phenomenon that arises from more basic natural rules, the way temperature arises from the motion of atoms, or the way wetness arises from water molecules. Real to us, but not a feature of the bedrock.

The Timeless Universe of Quantum Gravity

Perhaps the most compelling reason to believe that time is illusory comes from the bizarre puzzles that arise when physicists try to meld quantum mechanics with general relativity. In Loop Quantum Gravity (LQG) — one of the main approaches to a theory of quantum gravity — time disappears from the equations entirely. Carlo Rovelli, a founding figure of LQG, argues that time is not one of the fundamental constituents of the cosmos. Instead, what exists is a network of relations among quantum events.

In the same spirit, the Wheeler–DeWitt equation — an attempt at a quantum description of the universe's state — contains no time variable at all. It establishes a "wavefunction of the universe" in which all of history is contained within a static, timeless structure. The universe doesn't evolve over time. It just is.

Time as a Relational Concept

If time isn't fundamental, why do we experience it so intensely — that relentless forward pull, that irreversibility?

The answer may lie in the relational nature of time. Rather than being an absolute stage against which events play out, time arises from the way components of a system interact with one another. When we observe change in one part of a system by comparing it to another — a pendulum, a heartbeat, a decaying atom — we are constructing a local conception of time. There is no universal clock ticking behind the scenes.

This view is central to Rovelli's Thermal Time Hypothesis, which proposes that time emerges from thermodynamic evolution. In systems with many degrees of freedom, what we call time — and how we experience it — depends on which part of the system we use as our internal clock. There is no single flow; there are countless possible flows, each local, each contingent.

The Quantum Source of Time

The idea of time as emergent also surfaces in string theory. In some formulations, time need not be one of the theory's basic ingredients — it can be derived from the dynamics of more fundamental, pre-geometric structures: vibrating strings, abstract quantum states, entanglement patterns between subsystems. Time, on this picture, is something the universe computes rather than something it inhabits.

This would imply that our classical understanding of time breaks down completely at the scale of quantum gravity. At that scale, there is no smooth flow. The concept of "before" and "after" loses its grip. What we take to be the passage of time is something we project onto an underlying reality that is, in itself, perfectly still.

The Illusion of Temporal Flow

Even the arrow of time — the fact that time seems to move in one direction and not the other — may be an emergent property rather than a fundamental one. Thermodynamics tells us that time's arrow points in the direction of increasing entropy, of growing disorder. But this directionality may itself be an emergent expression of probabilistic rules and correlations between events, not a property written into the laws of the universe at the deepest level.

Time doesn't pass — we do. The so-called "flow of time" might be nothing more than the human brain tracking changes in an otherwise unchanging universe.

The sensation of "now" — that vivid, insistent present — may be entirely a matter of how consciousness functions within a vast, static web of associations and possibilities. Physics gives us no "now." It gives us a four-dimensional block in which all events — past, present, future — simply exist. The feeling of moving through them is ours alone.

Rethinking Reality

If these ideas hold, then time isn't a canvas upon which events are painted — it is a mental structure by which we make sense of experience. A useful fiction. A scaffold that macroscopic creatures like us need in order to navigate a world that is, at its most fundamental, something far stranger and more still.

As physicists continue to probe the nature of space, matter, and consciousness, we may find that the ultimate secrets of the universe lie beyond time. And in doing so, we are called upon to rethink not only physics, but the very nature of reality — and of ourselves as beings who experience it.
